Sir William Siemens Challenge

In the first semester of my third year at university, I was accepted into the Sir William Siemens Challenge, which is organised by the Siemens talent acquisition team, and is hosted in the iForge, the university’s student makerspace. The challenge is spread across 3 days, and involves being put into a multidisciplinary engineering team with students from other universities around the country.

The challenge we faced was to design, manufacture and present a system capable of displaying data captured by the Siemens MindSphere technology installed around the Faculty of Engineering’s Diamond building in a creative and innovate way. This data included readings from temperature, Co2, noise, humidity and population sensors.

A couple of weeks before the event, we were introduced to our team and were able to start communicating ideas and coming up with a plan for the challenge. We decided that we wanted to design and build an ambitious marble run, where the data could be represented by factors such as the paths the marbles took, how frequently the marbles are returned to the start of the run etc.

I was responsible for designing the marble run elevation system and the track in CAD, ensuring that it can be manufactured using the tools and equipment the iForge has, and using the materials that are available. The first hurdle to overcome was to design an elevation system that could repeatedly take marbles back to the top of the track. Initial ideas were based around a pulley system which used a belt with platforms attached at regular intervals for the marbles to sit on. However, we realised pretty early on that it was not a design that could be easily or reliably produced in the time we had, so I came up with a simple, easy to manufacture elevation system.

My elevator design contained a fixed chute for the marble to travel up, and a rotating blade that pushed the marbles up the chute. By ending the marble run at the base of the chute, the marbles could autonomously circulate round the track, and the frequency of marbles could be controlled by the speed of the rotating blade. The first design of the elevation system worked most of the time, but would occasionally stall the motor as the turbine blades contacted the marbles at quite a steep angle. To improve the design, the first half of the chute was modified with a curved profile so that the marble was gradually pushed in the upwards direction, reducing the stress on the motor.

Other parts of the marble run were then designed, such as a cool spiral track using clear tubing, with a continuous string of NeoPixel LEDs to generate some cool lighting animations. At the top of the chute, a splitter was designed to guide the incoming marbles in two different directions, using a servo motor that could shut off one lane at a time. The marbles could either be directed down the spiral tubing, or onto a series of straight acrylic tracks. The first part of the acrylic track had a series of solenoids, that would randomly fire, potentially pushing marble onto the track below – a feature that was supposed to represent the noise around the diamond (as the solenoids were very loud). Both the spiral and straight track combined at the bottom of the marble run, into a funnel that stored the marbles and fed them individually back into the elevator chute.

All the electromechanical devices and the LEDs were controlled using a Teensy 3 microcontroller, which was programmed in the Arduino environment. All the electronics were soldered onto a sheet of vero board, which was stored under the base of the marble run.

Most of the parts were manufactured using the iForge laser cutters, which meant the final system fitted together exactly as design in CAD. The main frame of the marble run was made using maker beam, which was provided in the kit, and the rest of the items were either bolted together or glued.

As the third and final day of work came to a close, the pressure to get everything completed and working for the final presentation was rising. A clumsy mistake meant one of the 5V power supplies got shorted out, killing the Teensy, which meant none of the LEDs, solenoids or motors controllers could be operated. With just minutes to spare, we decided to at least get the marble run working, by hooking up the turbines geared DC brushed motors to a buck regulator - even if there wasn’t going to be any pretty lighting effects or solenoid action.

Our time to present came along, and thankfully, whilst we were explaining our design process and team roles to the panel of judges and other teams, the marble run continued to operate flawlessly in the background, albeit a little loud.

Whilst we didn’t win the challenge, the event was a fantastic opportunity to develop friendships, network with other students and Siemens graduates, and learn some new skills. Furthermore, I was personally offered a 12-week internship with Siemens as result of the competition.

Later on in the year, the iForge wanted to put our marble run on display. This gave us the chance to re-design some aspects of the elevator mechanism and track. We spent several hours designing and manufacturing new parts of the marble run, and enclosed the entire thing in a clear acrylic container so that people can operate it whilst on display.
